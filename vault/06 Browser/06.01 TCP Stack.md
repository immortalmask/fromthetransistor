# 06.01 TCP Stack

## Why this block exists

Applications want an ordered byte stream, while the network supplies packets that may be lost, duplicated, delayed, truncated, or reordered. A constrained TCP implementation makes the bookkeeping behind “reliable” explicit.

## Lecture

### Mental model

Model one connection as two directional sequence spaces plus a protocol state. Sequence numbers name byte positions; SYN and FIN each consume one position. The sender retains unacknowledged bytes, limits transmission by the effective window, and retransmits on deterministic timer events. The receiver accepts bytes in its window, advances only across contiguous data, acknowledges the next expected sequence, and exposes ordered bytes to the socket.

Build under a deterministic packet simulator before a localhost adapter. Keep segment parsing, connection lookup, state transition, send queue, reassembly queue, and timer handling separate. Compare sequence numbers with modular arithmetic, not ordinary signed comparisons.

### Invariants

- `SND.UNA <= SND.NXT` in modular sequence-space terms, and retained bytes cover that interval.
- `RCV.NXT` names the first missing byte; delivered data below it is never delivered twice.
- A segment is parsed and checksum-validated before it mutates connection state.
- Every state transition is allowed by one explicit transition table.
- Timers are tied to protocol events and a simulated clock, never nondeterministic sleeps.

### Safety and limits

Use packet fixtures and localhost only. Bound connection count, segment length, reassembly bytes, retries, and trace size. The course subset deliberately omits congestion-control completeness, urgent data, advanced options, IPv6, TLS, and production hardening; do not expose it to hostile networks.

## Workbook

Trace a connection through SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK, two data segments delivered in reverse order, a duplicate, a lost ACK, retransmission, FIN, and close. Before simulation, predict and record state, `SND.UNA`, `SND.NXT`, `RCV.NXT`, send-queue bytes, reassembly intervals, and timer after every packet.

Repeat one comparison near sequence wraparound: place `SND.UNA` at `0xfffffff8`, send 16 bytes, and determine which ACKs are old, acceptable, or beyond sent data.

## Problem set

1. Calculate sequence and acknowledgment numbers for a handshake, 12 data bytes, and FIN in both directions.
2. Explain why acknowledging the highest observed byte rather than the first missing byte loses data under reordering.
3. Write a modular “before” relation and state the assumption that makes it unambiguous.
4. Design receiver behavior for exact duplicate, partial overlap, out-of-window data, bad checksum, and zero-length ACK.
5. Trace retransmission timeout with a lost data segment and a lost acknowledgment; identify which bytes remain retained.
6. Find the resource-exhaustion risk in unlimited out-of-order buffering and define a deterministic eviction/rejection rule.

## Homework

Implement: (1) bounded segment codec and checksum; (2) connection tuple lookup and transition table; (3) handshake; (4) ordered send/receive queues and ACK processing; (5) reassembly and duplicate suppression; (6) simulated timers/retransmission; (7) close states and socket-facing calls; (8) seeded loss, duplication, reorder, and wraparound scenarios.

Acceptance rubric: 20% parsing/state safety, 25% sequence and ACK correctness, 20% loss/reorder recovery, 10% flow/window behavior, 10% close handling, 10% deterministic tests, and 5% trace explanation. Success on a clean handshake alone is insufficient.

## Reverse-engineering lens

Analyze a packet capture with one hidden implementation defect. Reconstruct both sequence spaces and the state transition timeline, then identify the earliest packet where observed behavior diverges from the protocol model.

## Exit criteria

- [ ] You can trace sequence space through data, retransmission, and FIN.
- [ ] Duplicate and reordered bytes are never delivered twice or out of order.
- [ ] All timers and faults are deterministic and replayable.
- [ ] The core passes hostile fixture tests without using a real network.
