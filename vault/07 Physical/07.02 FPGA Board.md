# 07.02 FPGA Board

## Why this block exists

A digital design cannot compensate for missing power rails, an unstable clock, incorrect configuration straps, or incompatible I/O voltage. This block teaches board review as a set of checkable contracts rather than as an invitation to improvise a difficult BGA build.

## Lecture

### Mental model

Review the board in dependency order: input power and protection; regulator rails and sequencing; decoupling and return paths; reference clock and reset; FPGA bank voltages; configuration storage and JTAG; then UART, storage, Ethernet, LEDs, and expansion. Every signal belongs to a voltage domain, has one intended driver policy, and maps to one package pin and one logical function.

Represent the design in a small declarative board manifest containing rails, parts, pins, nets, interfaces, constraints, and required tests. A linter catches duplicate pins, missing grounds, incompatible voltage domains, unpowered banks, and absent pull resistors before fabrication.

### Invariants

- Every powered pin belongs to the correct named rail with local decoupling evidence.
- Every FPGA I/O pin appears at most once and its bank voltage supports the interface standard.
- No net has multiple push-pull drivers unless explicitly switched or isolated.
- Clock, reset, configuration, and JTAG remain reachable before application logic works.
- Schematic symbols, PCB footprints, BOM entries, and constraints refer to the same package variant.

### Safety and limits

The core deliverable is a reviewed manifest and known development-board mapping. Custom mains wiring, improvised ovens, lead exposure, hot-air/BGA work, and unsupervised first power are outside scope. A custom PCB requires experienced electrical review, manufacturer rules, current-limited supplies, eye protection, ventilation, and a written stop condition.

## Workbook

Audit a supplied manifest with seeded faults: swapped bank voltage, duplicated pin, missing ground, undriven reset, reversed connector, absent clock constraint, and two output drivers. Predict the linter result and physical symptom before running it.

Build a power-up dependency graph and mark which measurements can be made safely before inserting or configuring the FPGA.

## Problem set

1. Assign UART, JTAG, SD, LEDs, and Ethernet-related signals to banks given voltage and capability constraints; explain every rejected pin.
2. Calculate a conservative decoupling inventory from a supplied vendor checklist and identify placement-critical parts.
3. Diagnose a board where JTAG works only while reset is held and list measurements in safest order.
4. Find all multi-driver and floating-input risks in a small net table.
5. Explain why a successful schematic ERC does not prove footprint, power-integrity, or assembly correctness.
6. Write pre-fabrication and first-power checklists with explicit pass/fail thresholds and stop conditions.

## Homework

Stage the artifact as: (1) machine-readable board manifest; (2) linter for pin uniqueness, rail compatibility, required nets, and driver ownership; (3) development-board pin mapping and constraints; (4) block diagram/BOM review report; (5) power-up and measurement plan; (6) optional schematic/PCB review package only after mentor approval.

Acceptance rubric: 25% manifest consistency, 20% electrical-domain checks, 15% boot/config/debug accessibility, 15% development-board mapping, 15% safety and first-power plan, and 10% seeded-fault tests. Fabrication is not required and cannot substitute for review evidence.

## Reverse-engineering lens

Given an unknown board's photos, continuity table, connector labels, and partial schematic, infer likely power domains and debug paths. Separate direct observations from hypotheses, and propose non-destructive measurements that reduce uncertainty.

## Exit criteria

- [ ] The declarative board mapping passes duplicate-pin, voltage, and ownership checks.
- [ ] Clock, reset, configuration, and JTAG paths are independently reviewable.
- [ ] A current-limited first-power checklist has thresholds and stop conditions.
- [ ] You can explain why the software course does not require custom-board fabrication.
